

After Playtest

Playtest revealed that Free Actions undermined the action economy, because players could perform too many meaningful operations outside the two-action limit. This reduced decision tension and created timing ambiguity.

Playtest also revealed that Influence scoring encouraged hoarding rather than spending, which reduced late-game interaction.

Finally, the original 52-card design created excessive cognitive load. Players could not remember or predict possible card effects, so cards failed to create meaningful bluffing or strategic counterplay.

1. The Action rules are too lenient. I believe the Free Action system renders the restrictions on Actions meaningless and creates significant ambiguity in the actual understanding of the rules. There are too many possible actions in a single turn: play card, contest, buy card, buy investors, deploy investors, move investors, refresh the market...

2. The final scoring system is unbalanced. For example, accumulating Influence in the late game is more useful than spending it. This also leads to players focusing too much on their resources rather than engaging in meaningful gameplay.

3. There are simply too many cards. There's no need for so many cards. Aside from the learning curve, they don't actually affect the "gameplay." I can't remember or predict what cards my opponent has. This reduces the game to a matter of territory control.
