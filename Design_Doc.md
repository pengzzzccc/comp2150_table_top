# Wolves of Wand Street

**Design Document**

---

## 1 · Target Experience

### High Concept

Four ambitious entrepreneurs set sail for a virgin continent, a land untouched by commerce, ripe for conquest. In *Wolves of Wand Street*, 3–4 players race to build financial empires across an open frontier, simultaneously deploying investors, claiming territories, and waging economic warfare in a fast-paced card-driven area-control game. With simultaneous action phases, independent draw piles, and a streamlined three-round structure, each game lasts approximately 10 minutes and delivers a concentrated burst of resource management, territorial strategy, and competitive brinkmanship.

### Fellowship

*Wolves of Wand Street* is a competitive free-for-all where interaction is not merely encouraged, it is structurally unavoidable. All players make decisions at the same time during the Income and Action phases, creating a shared tension at the table: everyone watches the Land Pool, anticipates rivals' moves, and races to execute their plans first. During the sequential Attack & Contest phase, direct confrontation takes centrestage: every contest for a land is a head-to-head clash between attacker and defender. Because players can target **any** land rather than only adjacent ones (a deliberate evolution from our original adjacency-based design, which created stalemates where players turtled behind frontlines), no one is ever safe. This open targeting rule forces constant vigilance and prevents passive play. The game encourages temporary alliances of convenience: two players may tacitly cooperate to weaken a leader, knowing that all alliances are ultimately self-serving. The randomised turn order each round prevents permanent king-making arrangements and ensures that initiative advantage shifts unpredictably.

### Challenge

The game presents two interlocking layers of challenge:

**Intellectual Challenge.** Each player operates their own 12-card draw pile, creating a unique card economy per player. With a hand limit of five and two distinct card sources (free Draw Cards and purchasable Market Cards), players must continuously evaluate whether to invest Gold in Market Cards for future turns or spend it immediately for immediate advantage. The resource conversion loop (play cards to gain Gold, spend Gold to buy Market Cards, deploy Investors onto territories) requires multi-turn planning compressed into just three rounds. Because all players act simultaneously, you cannot react to opponents' actions in real time, so you must anticipate their likely moves and prepare accordingly.

**Social Challenge.** Timing aggression is critical. During the Action phase, players must read the table: who is accumulating Investors? Who is eyeing the King's Castle? And decide whether to contest aggressively or consolidate. The sequential Attack & Contest phase introduces turn-order dynamics: going first lets you claim contested lands before others, but going last lets you react to the board state. Holding Market Cards like *Political Coup* or *Corporate Espionage* creates implicit threats that shape opponents' decisions even before they are played.

### Drama

The dramatic arc follows a three-act structure compressed into three intense rounds:

- **Round 1 (Foundation).** Players claim lands from the Land Pool, deploy Investors, and establish their economic engines. Tension is moderate: the King's Castle (worth 3 Land Points) looms as the ultimate prize, but claiming it requires committing a scarce Investor early.
- **Round 2 (Escalation).** The first contests erupt. Market Cards enter play: *Hostile Takeover* inflates attack strength, *Fortify* locks down key territories, *Market Crash* devastates a leader's Gold reserves. Power balances shift with each engagement. Players who invested in Market Cards now wield dramatic advantages.
- **Round 3 (Desperation).** Every action matters. Players must decide: contest for lands to maximise Land Points, or conserve Gold for the tiebreaker? You must commit to your plan and hope it outperforms theirs. The final scoring reveals winners and losers in a single dramatic moment.

The **independent draw piles** create personal tension: each player's deck is finite (12 cards), and exhaustion is visible. Running out of cards in Round 3 means fewer options, forcing players to pace their card usage across all three rounds.

### Fantasy

The game's form supports its fiction through consistent thematic layering. The title *Wolves of Wand Street* merges the predatory energy of Wall Street with the arcane ("Wand"), grounding the setting in a world where finance and sorcery are one and the same. Gold functions as both currency and magical resource: lands produce it like revenue streams, and Investors are deployed like corporate assets. Card names reinforce the economic warfare theme: "Hostile Takeover," "Corporate Espionage," "Market Crash," and "Tax Collection" all evoke ruthless capitalism. The Land Pool structure (Hamlets as small ventures, Thriving Cities as established businesses, the King's Castle as the ultimate market monopoly) maps the city-state metaphor onto the board. The simultaneous action format mirrors the chaos of a real market: everyone acts at once, and only afterwards do you discover who outmanoeuvred whom.

## 2 · Core Gameplay

The game is built on three interlocking systems: the **Economy System**, the **Land & Deployment System**, and the **Contest (Combat) System**. A fourth supporting system, the **Market Card System**, layers strategic depth on top of these foundations. Each system creates a distinct type of decision tension, and their interactions ensure that no single strategy dominates.

### 2.1 The Economy System

**Mechanics.** Each round opens with an **Income Phase**: all players gain 3 Gold, collect Investor income from their lands (Hamlet = 1, Thriving City = 2, King's Castle = 3), and draw 2 cards from their personal draw pile. Gold is spent on Market Cards (costs range from 2–6 Gold) and serves as a secondary tiebreaker at game end (Gold ÷ 5, rounded down).

**Design Intent.** Gold creates a **conversion dilemma** that recurs every round. Every Gold spent on a Market Card is Gold that cannot contribute to your tiebreaker score. The fixed 3 Gold base income ensures all players start each round with a minimum purchasing power, while land-based Investor income rewards territorial control with more deployment options. The per-player draw pile means card draw is predictable and controllable: you know exactly which 12 cards your deck contains, allowing strategic planning across all three rounds.

**System Interaction.** Economy feeds Land & Deployment by enabling Market Card purchases (which can generate Investors or protect territories), but is gated by the Card System: you must draw the right cards and have Gold available. This three-way dependency means economic investment and territorial expansion must be carefully balanced.

### 2.2 The Land & Deployment System

**Mechanics.** The game uses a shared **Land Pool** containing 4 Hamlets and 1 King's Castle (the two starting Thriving Cities are already in players' setups). Each player begins with 1 Thriving City and 1 Hamlet, each defended by 2 Investors. During the Action phase, players may:
- Deploy Investors from their hand onto lands they own.
- Move deployed Investors between their own lands.
- Claim a free land from the Land Pool by placing 1 Investor on it.

Land values are: Hamlet = 1 Land Point (produces 1 Investor/turn), Thriving City = 2 Land Points (produces 2 Investors/turn), King's Castle = 3 Land Points (produces 3 Investors/turn).

**Design Intent.** The Land Pool replaces a fixed map with **dynamic territory selection**. Players choose which lands to claim based on their current needs: early Hamlets are cheap and safe, while the King's Castle is high-value but highly contested. The fact that Investors are placed from the **hand** (not a reserve) means every deployment is a choice between defensive strength and offensive capability. Turn order breaks ties in purchasing, but claiming from the Land Pool happens during the sequential Attack & Contest phase, adding strategic timing pressure.

**System Interaction.** The Land system feeds Economy by generating Investor income, which feeds back into card draw and deployment. Land modifiers (Minor Export, Branch Office) can permanently enhance a territory's output, creating investment incentives that interact with the contest system: a modified land is more valuable to capture.

### 2.3 The Contest (Combat) System

**Mechanics.** During the sequential Attack & Contest phase (turn order determined randomly each round), each player may contest up to 1 land per turn. To contest:
1. Commit Investors from your hand to attack any land on any player's board (no adjacency restriction).
2. Compare Investor counts: **higher total wins**. **Defender wins ties** and keeps their land.
3. Both sides lose Investors at a 1:1 ratio (placed back into Investor piles, **not** hands).
4. The winning attacker's surviving Investors become the new defenders of the captured land and **cannot contest again this turn**.
5. Additional Investors from the attacker's hand may be placed on the captured land.

Market Cards can modify contests: *Hostile Takeover* adds +2 virtual Investors to your first contest; *Rapid Expansion* (a Draw Card) allows contesting 2 lands instead of 1.

**Design Intent.** The contest system is deliberately simple (pure Investor count comparison) to keep the focus on strategic resource allocation rather than combat mechanics. The 1:1 casualty rule creates meaningful attrition: even a successful attack depletes your forces. The defender-wins-ties rule makes aggression costly, encouraging deliberate build-ups rather than reckless assaults. The "any land" targeting rule (no adjacency restriction) means no player is ever safe, maintaining constant tension. The fact that surviving attackers become defenders creates a consequential deployment decision: commit too many Investors to attack and you leave your own lands vulnerable; commit too few and you lose the contest.

**System Interaction.** Contests consume Investors from the hand, directly competing with deployment and claiming actions for the same limited resource. Market Cards that enhance combat (Hostile Takeover, Fortify) interact with the economy system through their Gold costs.

### 2.4 The Market Card System

**Mechanics.** A shared Market displays 4 face-up cards from the Market pile (ranks J–A). During the Action phase, any player may buy a Market Card by paying its Gold cost. If two or more players want the same card, the player with the earliest turn order buys it. Bought cards go directly into the hand but **cannot be played on the turn they are bought**: they enter play when next drawn. Market refresh costs 2 Gold. Market Cards span four suits:
- **♦ Diamonds (Operations):** Military enhancements (*Hostile Takeover, Strike Action, Fortify, Angel Investors*).
- **♥ Hearts (Manipulation):** Direct player interaction (*Hostile Negotiation, Corporate Espionage, Political Coup, Power Play*).
- **♠ Spades (Economy):** Economic warfare (*Market Crash, Tax Collection, Monopoly, Economic Dominance*).
- **♣ Clubs (Arcane):** Card and resource manipulation (*Arcane Recession, Mystical Arbitrage, Time Acceleration, Arcane Surge*).

**Design Intent.** Market Cards provide **asymmetric strategic options** beyond the shared Draw Card pool. Their delayed-play mechanic (cannot be used on the purchase turn) prevents instant gratification and forces multi-turn planning. The four-suit structure creates distinct strategic archetypes: aggressive (♦), disruptive (♥), economic (♠), and arcane (♣). The face-up display creates information: all players can see what is available and anticipate who might buy what, while the turn-order tiebreaker adds a competitive dimension to purchasing.

**System Interaction.** Market Cards are the primary mechanism through which Gold converts into strategic advantage. They interact with every other system: Operations cards affect contests, Manipulation cards affect opponents, Economy cards affect Gold generation, and Arcane cards affect card draw and resources.

### Additional Mechanics

**Turn Order Dice/Chips.** Turn order for the Attack & Contest phase is randomised each round, preventing permanent first-player advantages and ensuring that initiative dynamics shift unpredictably.

**Independent Draw Piles.** Each player has their own 12-card draw pile, eliminating the randomness of a shared deck and allowing players to plan their card usage across all three rounds. Exhaustion is permanent: if your draw pile is empty, draws are skipped.

**Land Modifiers.** Cards like *Minor Export* (+1 Gold/turn) and *Branch Office* (+1 Investor/turn) can be permanently placed on lands. These modifiers stack and transfer on capture, creating investment decisions that interact with the contest system.

## 3 · Interesting Decisions

The game produces several interlocking interesting decisions. The following five are the most strategically significant.

### 3.1 The King's Castle Dilemma: Claim Now or Wait?

The King's Castle (3 Land Points, 3 Investors/turn) is the most valuable land in the pool, deliberately designed as a high-value focal point to drive player interaction and contesting. Its dominance is intentional: by creating a single premium target, the game guarantees that players will clash over it rather than peacefully expanding into separate territories. The question is not whether to contest the Castle, but when and at what cost.

1. **Balanced:** Claiming early grants the highest income but paints a target on your back. Waiting lets others claim it and potentially fortify it. Ignoring it entirely means accepting a Land Point disadvantage. Playtesting confirmed this tension: players who rushed the Castle were frequently contested, while those who waited sometimes lost it permanently.
2. **Informed:** All players can see the Land Pool and know the King's Castle's value. However, opponents' hand sizes and Investor counts are partially hidden: you can see deployed Investors but not those in hand.
3. **Consequential:** Claiming the King's Castle in Round 1 gives you 2 extra Investors per turn for Rounds 2 and 3, a compounding advantage. But losing it to a contest wastes your initial Investor and gives the attacker a massive boost.
4. **Situational:** If you go first in the Attack & Contest phase, claiming the King's Castle is safer (you act before potential attackers). If you go last, it may be wiser to claim a Hamlet first and contest the Castle later.

### 3.2 Market Card Investment vs. Gold Conservation

Each round, players must decide whether to spend Gold on Market Cards or conserve it for the endgame tiebreaker.

1. **Balanced:** Market Cards provide powerful abilities (e.g., *Hostile Takeover* effectively adds +2 Investors to a contest), but their costs (2–6 Gold) significantly reduce your tiebreaker score. Conserving Gold preserves tiebreaker value but leaves you without asymmetric advantages.
2. **Informed:** The Market displays 4 face-up cards, so all players can see what is available. You know your own hand and draw pile composition, allowing precise calculation of what you need.
3. **Consequential:** Spending 5 Gold on *Angel Investors* gives 4 immediate Investors, potentially game-changing for a contest. But that 5 Gold, divided by 5, is 1 lost tiebreaker point. In a close game, that point could decide the winner.
4. **Situational:** In Round 1, Market Card investment builds long-term advantage. In Round 3, Gold conservation is usually superior since there is no future turn to benefit from Market Cards. The optimal pivot point is Round 2.

### 3.3 Investor Deployment: Hand vs. Board

Players must decide when to deploy Investors from their hand onto lands versus keeping them available for contests.

1. **Balanced:** Deployed Investors defend lands and generate visible strength, but are locked in place (except via *Redeployment* cards). Investors in hand are flexible: they can attack any land or defend by being deployed reactively, but don't contribute to defence until deployed.
2. **Informed:** All deployed Investors are visible. Hand contents are hidden, creating information asymmetry. An opponent with many deployed Investors is strong on defence but may be vulnerable on offence.
3. **Consequential:** Deploying all Investors onto lands maximises defence but leaves you unable to contest. Keeping all in hand maximises offensive potential but leaves your lands vulnerable to undefended capture.
4. **Situational:** If you are the turn-order leader (act first in Attack & Contest), keeping Investors in hand for aggressive contests is viable. If you act last, deploying for defence may be wiser since others may contest your lands before you can act.

### 3.4 Draw Card Timing: When to Use Redeployment

The *Redeployment* card (×3 in each draw pile) retrieves up to 2 deployed Investors back into your hand. Deciding when to use it is critical.

1. **Balanced:** Using *Redeployment* early frees Investors for offence but weakens your defences. Using it late allows reactive defence adjustments but may come too late to matter. Holding it creates a threat that opponents must account for.
2. **Informed:** You know which lands have how many deployed Investors. You can assess which territories are likely to be contested based on opponents' visible board states.
3. **Consequential:** Retrieving 2 Investors from a lightly defended land could free enough force to contest the King's Castle. But that land is now at 0 Investors: any opponent can claim it automatically.
4. **Situational:** *Redeployment* is most valuable when you have committed too many Investors to one land and need flexibility elsewhere. In Round 3, it becomes a last-resort tool for maximising your final contest.

### 3.5 Simultaneous Action Reading: Anticipating Opponents

Because the Action phase is simultaneous, players must anticipate opponents' moves without seeing them in real time.

1. **Balanced:** Aggressive strategies (buying Market Cards, deploying for attack) are powerful but risky if opponents do the same. Conservative strategies (claiming Hamlets, hoarding Gold) are safe but may fall behind.
2. **Informed:** You can see opponents' board states (deployed Investors, controlled lands, Gold income) and the Market display. You cannot see their hands or their exact plans.
3. **Consequential:** If you anticipate an opponent will contest your King's Castle, you can deploy extra Investors for defence. If you guess wrong, you've wasted deployment on a land that wasn't attacked while leaving other lands exposed.
4. **Situational:** In Round 1, there is less information to read: opponents' strategies are still forming. By Round 3, patterns are clear and anticipation becomes critical. The player who best reads the table wins.

## 4 · Adding Variety

Several mechanisms ensure that each playthrough feels distinct:

**Independent Draw Piles.** Each player's 12-card draw pile is shuffled individually. The order in which Draw Cards appear varies, creating different resource and action sequences per player. One player may draw *Rapid Expansion* early and contest aggressively; another may draw *Minor Export* and build an economic engine.

**Randomised Turn Order.** The Attack & Contest turn order is randomised each round, preventing fixed positional advantages and creating fresh tactical dynamics. Going first in one round means potentially going last in the next.

**Land Pool Dynamics.** With only 4 Hamlets and 1 King's Castle available for claiming, the Land Pool creates a competitive scarcity. Which lands get claimed, and by whom, varies with player strategies and turn order.

**Market Card Availability.** The 4 face-up Market Cards change as players buy them. The randomness of which cards appear, and which players can afford them, creates different strategic opportunities each game.

**Player-Driven Variability.** The simultaneous format and free-for-all structure mean player interactions are emergent. Temporary alliances, target selection, and aggression timing shift with player personalities and in-game dynamics. No two groups will play the same game.

**Draw Pile Exhaustion Timing.** Since each player has only 12 cards, the pace of card usage varies. Players who cycle aggressively may exhaust their draw pile by Round 3, limiting their options. Conservative players retain options but may miss early opportunities.

## 6 · Production Process

### Tools

- **playingcards.io:** The primary digital implementation and online playtesting platform. The `.pcio` file contains the complete game with automated setup buttons, custom card zones, Investor token management, and Land Pool display.
- **LaTeX and Python Scripts:** An automated card asset generation pipeline. Card definitions are processed through scripts to produce PNG card images with consistent formatting, which are then imported into playingcards.io.
- **Paper Prototyping:** Physical prototypes were essential for testing the simultaneous action format and the streamlined contest mechanics. Standard playing cards and tokens were used for early iterations. A quick-reference rules summary card was added after Playtest 2 revealed initial comprehension issues.
- **Git and GitHub:** Version control for all design documents, rules, assets, and the `.pcio` file. The repository history tracks the evolution from v1 (sequential, 52-card) to v0.2 (sequential, 48-card) to the final Simultaneous Quickplay edition.

### Team Workflow

The team comprised four members (James, Louis, Marcus, and Aidan) with the following division of labour:

- **Louis** authored the initial v1 rule set, providing the foundational game structure.
- **Aidan** led the visual design for all card artwork, creating the custom card illustrations and graphic design. All team members contributed to card production.
- **James, Louis, Aidan, and Marcus** collectively iterated on the rules through three major design phases: v1 (complex sequential) to v0.2 (simplified sequential) to Super Quickplay Simultaneous Edition. Each iteration was driven by playtesting feedback.
- **All members** participated in playtesting, physical prototype construction, and rule refinement throughout the project.

### Timeline

| Phase | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Concept & v1 Design** | Louis designed the initial 52-card sequential game with Influence scoring, Free Actions, and complex card effects. |
| **Paper Prototype & Early Playtesting** | Physical prototype built and tested. Multiple playtests identified critical issues: Free Actions undermined action economy, Influence encouraged hoarding, and excessive cognitive load from unique cards. |

| **Final Playtesting & Balancing** | Three playtests conducted (5/7, 5/21, 5/21) with 11 total participants. Data-driven iteration: dice variance removed, quick-reference rules added, turtling identified as the primary remaining balance issue. |

## 7 · Reflection

### Playtest Summary

We conducted three playtests with a total of 11 participants, using a structured questionnaire (Likert scale 1 to 5) and open-ended feedback.

| Metric | Playtest 1 (v1, 7 players, 5/7) | Playtest 2 (Simultaneous, 2 players, 5/21) | Playtest 3 (Simultaneous, 2 players, 5/21) |
|--------|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Rules easy to understand | 4.1 | 2.0 | 4.5 |
| Cards easy to understand | N/A | 5.0 | 4.0 |
| Player agency/freedom | 2.4 | N/A | 2.5 |
| Game pacing | 2.6 | 2.5 | 4.0 |
| Options to stop a leader | 2.6 | 3.0 | 2.0 |

**Key findings:** Playtest 1 revealed that the original dice-based resource system created too much variance (multiple players cited randomness as the worst part) and that player agency was low (average 2.4/5). Playtest 2 showed the new simultaneous rules were initially confusing (2.0/5 for rules clarity), but cards were immediately clear (5.0/5). Playtest 3, after providing a quick-reference rules summary, resolved the clarity issue (4.5/5 rules, 4.0/5 pacing) but exposed a persistent problem: turtling was dominant. One player reported that "turtling up and building a big defence was tooooooooooooooo good" and the game "needs more aggression." The ability to stop a leader scored only 2.0/5 in Playtest 3, confirming that defensive strategies are currently too strong.

### Strengths

The **simultaneous action format** is the design's greatest innovation. By making Income and Action phases happen at the same time, the game eliminates downtime entirely: all players are engaged at every moment. This creates a unique planning tension: you must commit to your strategy without knowing what opponents will do, then watch the Attack & Contest phase reveal who outmanoeuvred whom.

The **independent draw pile** system gives each player agency over their card economy. Knowing that your deck contains exactly 12 specific cards allows strategic planning across all three rounds, a depth of planning unusual in a 10-minute game. The per-player design also eliminates the randomness of a shared deck, ensuring that your draws depend on your shuffling, not others' actions.

The **simplified contest mechanic** (pure Investor count comparison with 1:1 casualties) keeps the focus on strategic resource allocation rather than combat resolution. The defender-wins-ties rule creates a natural defensive advantage that encourages deliberate build-ups rather than reckless attacks, while the open targeting rule ensures no one is ever safe.

The **three-round structure** with escalating stakes creates a natural dramatic arc. Each round feels distinct: Round 1 is about building, Round 2 is about contesting, and Round 3 is about desperate final plays.

### Areas for Improvement

**Turtling is too effective.** Playtest 3 data (agency 2.5/5, ability to stop a leader 2.0/5) confirms that defensive strategies dominate. Players who hoard Investors on a few key lands become nearly impossible to dislodge. Potential solutions include: increasing the casualty ratio for defenders, adding cards that bypass defences (e.g., *Strike Action* already returns Investors to hand), or limiting the number of Investors that can defend a single land.

**Player agency feels low.** Across all three playtests, agency scores ranged from 2.0 to 2.5/5. Players feel constrained by their small hand size and limited actions. Increasing the hand limit from 5 to 6, or allowing a third action in Round 3, could give players more room to execute strategies.

**Information lag in simultaneous play.** Players who act faster during the Action phase may gain advantages by claiming contested Market Cards before opponents can react. Clear timing rules or a structured simultaneous resolution mechanism may be needed.

### Lessons Learned

**Simplicity is the hardest design achievement.** The evolution from v1 (52 unique cards, sequential play, complex effects) to the Simultaneous Quickplay Edition (12-card draw piles, simultaneous actions, pure Investor contests) required removing features at every iteration. Each removal solved a playtesting problem and improved the player experience.

**Format shapes experience.** The shift from sequential to simultaneous play fundamentally changed the game's social dynamic, from a turn-by-turn strategy game to a shared-tension racing game. The format itself is a design choice with profound implications.

**Playtesting is irreplaceable.** Problems that seemed invisible during design (dice variance, Influence hoarding, turtling, pacing) only surfaced through structured playtesting with external participants. The gap between designer intent and player experience can only be bridged through external testing.

- Attacker wins. Both sides lose 4 Investors. Land goes to Attacker.

## Appendix C: Quick Reference Card

### Round Structure

| Phase | Who | Actions |
|-------|-----|---------|
| **INCOME** | All players (simultaneous) | Gain 3 Gold. Collect Investor income from lands. Draw 2 cards. |
| **ACTION** | All players (simultaneous) | Buy Market Cards (Gold cost). Play non-[Attack] cards. Deploy Investors onto lands. Move Investors between your own lands. Place [Attack] cards face-down. Optionally discard 2 cards to draw 1. |
| **ATTACK & CONTEST** | In turn order (sequential) | Flip [Attack] cards. Contest up to 1 land. Claim 1 land from Pool. |
| **DISCARD** | All players | Hand limit: 5 cards. Discard excess. |

### Contest Resolution

1. Attacker commits Investors from hand.
2. Compare totals: **Higher wins. Defender wins ties.**
3. Both sides lose Investors at 1:1 ratio: back to Investor piles (not hands).
4. Surviving attackers become defenders of captured land.
5. Undefended land (0 Investors): automatic capture, place ≥1 Investor.

### Scoring (End of Round 3)

| Component | Calculation |
|-----------|-------------|
| Land Points | Hamlet = 1, Thriving City = 2, King's Castle = 3 |
| Gold | Remaining Gold ÷ 5 (round down) |
| **Final Score** | **Land Points + Gold ÷ 5** |
| Tiebreakers | Most lands → Most Investors on map → Most Gold |
